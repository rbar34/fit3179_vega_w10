Asylum Seekers, Immigrants and Foreign Born Population

Where do Asylum Seekers Come From and Who Takes their Fair Share?

Asylum seekers are people who leave their home countries, but whose refugee status has not been determined yet. A person can become an asylum seeker by leaving their home country if it is no longer safe for them to live there. Countries can become inhospitable for their citizens for a range of reasons including war, corruption, extreme poverty, persecution or climate crises.

The OECD countries represented in the emoji scatterplot below are some of the countries who absorb asylum seeker who are found to be refugees. The scatterplot shows the trend of asylum seeker intake against OECD nation population, revealing that intake is proportional to the population of the destination country. Over time, all countries seem to increase their asylum seeker intake. What is the Proportion of Foreign Born Citizens in OECD Nations?

Foreign-born citizens are often just economic immigrants and not resettled refugees. The choropleth maps below give an indication of the ethnic diversity of each state, territory, region or country. The scales the three maps are the same so comparisons can be made between them.

The United States and Canada have accommodated several waves of immigration. Their most diverse states are California and Ontario.

Europe is comprised of several non-OECD nations which is why this graph shows missing data. The most diverse country in Europe is Luxembourg with nearly 45% foreign-born population.

Australia is regarded as a multi-cultural country, with most states having a higher foreign-born population than those in North America and Europe. West Australia has the highest proportion of foreign born population here.
